Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (18 Marks)

Section B: Short Answer Questions (14 Marks)

Instructions: Answer in 30-40 words each.

  1. Define ‘Hegemony’ in the context of international relations.
    Expected Answer: Hegemony refers to the dominance of one state over others in political, economic, or military spheres, often exercised through influence rather than direct control, e.g., US hegemony post-Cold War.
  2. What was the significance of the Non-Aligned Movement during the Cold War?
    Expected Answer: It allowed countries like India to avoid superpower blocs, maintain sovereignty, and promote peace by fostering cooperation among newly independent nations.
  3. Explain ‘Federalism’ as it applies to Indian politics.
    Expected Answer: Federalism in India involves a division of powers between the Centre and states, with a strong Centre, as outlined in the Constitution, balancing unity and regional diversity.
  4. List the main objectives of the United Nations.
    Expected Answer: Maintain international peace and security, promote cooperation, protect human rights, and provide humanitarian aid.
  5. What was the Green Revolution in India?
    Expected Answer: A 1960s initiative to increase agricultural productivity through high-yield seeds, fertilizers, and irrigation, reducing food scarcity.
  6. What is meant by ‘Panchsheel’ in India’s foreign policy?
    Expected Answer: Panchsheel refers to five principles of peaceful coexistence, including mutual respect and non-interference, guiding India’s relations with other nations.
  7. Describe the concept of ‘Globalisation’.
    Expected Answer: Globalisation is the process of increasing interconnectedness through trade, culture, and technology, impacting economies and societies worldwide.

Section C: Questions (20 Marks)

Instructions: Answer in 100-120 words each.

  1. Discuss the impact of globalization on developing countries.
    Expected Answer: Globalization has boosted trade and technology access in developing countries, fostering economic growth (e.g., India’s IT sector). However, it has also widened inequality, exploited labor, and eroded cultural identities due to Western influence.
  2. Analyze the role of the United Nations in maintaining international peace and security.
    Expected Answer: The UN mediates conflicts (e.g., Cyprus peacekeeping), imposes sanctions, and authorizes interventions. Its effectiveness is limited by veto power in the Security Council and lack of enforcement mechanisms.
  3. Explain the challenges faced by India during the era of one-party dominance.
    Expected Answer: From 1947-1967, Congress dominance faced challenges like opposition weakness, internal factionalism, and managing diversity (language, caste), yet it ensured stability and nation-building.
  4. Compare and contrast the foreign policies of India and China with their neighbors.
    Expected Answer: India emphasizes non-alignment and peaceful coexistence (e.g., Panchsheel with China), while China pursues assertive policies (e.g., Belt and Road). Both seek regional influence but differ in approach—India diplomatic, China economic/military.
  5. Evaluate the effectiveness of the Indian Constitution in addressing social inequalities.
    Expected Answer: The Constitution provides reservations and fundamental rights to reduce inequalities (e.g., caste, gender). Successes include upliftment of marginalized groups, but challenges like poverty and discrimination persist.

Section D: Passage, Cartoon, and Map-Based Questions (18 Marks)

1. Passage-Based Question (6 Marks)

Passage: "The European Union (EU) is a political and economic union of 27 member states. It promotes a single market and common policies, but faces challenges like Brexit and economic disparities among members."

  1. What are the main objectives of the European Union? (2 marks)
    Expected Answer: Promote peace, economic integration, and cooperation through a single market and shared policies.
  2. Discuss the challenges faced by the EU in recent years. (4 marks)
    Expected Answer: Brexit disrupted unity, economic gaps between rich (e.g., Germany) and poor (e.g., Greece) nations strain cohesion, and rising nationalism challenges integration.

2. Cartoon-Based Question (6 Marks)

Description: A cartoon shows two figures labeled ‘USA’ and ‘USSR’ arm wrestling, with smaller nations watching nervously.

  1. What does this cartoon represent in the context of the Cold War? (3 marks)
    Expected Answer: It depicts the intense rivalry and power struggle between the USA and USSR, with smaller nations caught in their ideological conflict.
  2. How does it relate to the concept of bipolarity? (3 marks)
    Expected Answer: Bipolarity refers to the division of global power into two blocs (USA and USSR), as shown by their dominance over smaller nations.

3. Map-Based Question (6 Marks)

Instructions: Imagine a blank map of South Asia is provided.

  1. Identify the country sharing borders with India, Pakistan, and China. (2 marks)
    Answer: Afghanistan
  2. Name the island nation that is a member of SAARC. (2 marks)
    Answer: Sri Lanka
  3. Locate the country formerly known as East Pakistan. (2 marks)
    Answer: Bangladesh

Section E: Long Answer Questions (10 Marks)

Instructions: Answer in 150-200 words each.

  1. Critically analyze the role of political parties in Indian democracy.
    Expected Answer: Political parties are vital to Indian democracy, mobilizing voters, forming governments, and shaping policies. Congress dominated initially, ensuring stability, while multi-party systems later reflected diversity (e.g., BJP, regional parties). However, issues like corruption, dynastic politics, and vote-bank strategies weaken democratic values. Their role in coalition governments shows adaptability but often compromises governance efficiency.
  2. Discuss the impact of coalition politics on governance in India.
    Expected Answer: Coalition politics, prominent since the 1990s, reflects India’s diversity but complicates governance. It ensures broader representation (e.g., NDA, UPA) but leads to policy delays, compromises, and instability due to conflicting interests. While it curbs authoritarianism, it often prioritizes political survival over development.
