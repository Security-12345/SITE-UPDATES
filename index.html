Part A: Contemporary World Politics

1. The Cold War Era

What It Was: After World War II, the US (capitalist) and Soviet Union (communist) competed for power without fighting directly.

  • Causes: Different ideas (capitalism vs. communism), distrust, power struggle.
  • Cuban Missile Crisis (1962): US and Soviet Union nearly started a nuclear war over missiles in Cuba.
  • Non-Aligned Movement (NAM): India and others stayed neutral.
  • Why Important: Expect questions on NAM or Cuban crisis.

2. The End of Bipolarity

What Happened: Soviet Union broke apart in 1991, ending the two-power world.

  • New countries like Russia formed.
  • US became the only superpower.
  • Global politics shifted to one dominant power.
  • Why Important: Questions on how this changed the world.

3. US Hegemony in World Politics

What It Means: One country (US) dominates others.

  • After 1991, US led decisions (e.g., Iraq wars).
  • Power from military, economy, culture (Hollywood).
  • Why Important: Questions on US power or actions.

4. Alternative Centres of Power

What It’s About: Others challenging US dominance.

  • China: Growing economy and military.
  • European Union (EU): Group of European countries.
  • ASEAN: Southeast Asian teamwork.
  • Why Important: Questions on China or EU.

5. Contemporary South Asia

What It Covers: Politics in India’s neighbors.

  • Pakistan: Democracy struggles.
  • Sri Lanka: Sinhalese vs. Tamil fights.
  • India’s Relations: Issues with Pakistan (Kashmir), China (borders).
  • Why Important: India’s role in South Asia.

6. International Organizations

What It’s About: Groups like the UN helping the world.

  • UN Structure: Security Council (5 permanent: US, UK, Russia, China, France), General Assembly.
  • UN Jobs: Peace, conflict resolution.
  • Reforms: Calls for UN changes.
  • Why Important: Questions on UN roles or members.

7. Security in the Contemporary World

What It Means: Safety from dangers.

  • Traditional Threats: Country wars.
  • Non-Traditional Threats: Terrorism, pandemics, climate change.
  • Why Important: New security issues in exams.

8. Environment and Natural Resources

What It Covers: Global environmental challenges.

  • Problems: Pollution, warming, deforestation.
  • Solutions: Agreements like Paris Agreement.
  • Why Important: Hot exam topic.

9. Globalization

What It Is: World getting connected.

  • Economic: Global trade, companies.
  • Cultural: Sharing movies, food, ideas.
  • Impact on India: Jobs but inequality too.
  • Why Important: Questions on India and globalization.

Part B: Politics in India since Independence

1. Challenges of Nation Building

What It Was: Making India one country after 1947.

  • Princely States: 500+ joined India (e.g., Hyderabad, Junagadh).
  • State Reorganization: Language-based states (e.g., Andhra Pradesh 1953).
  • Diversity: Managing religions, cultures.
  • Why Important: Map or integration questions.

2. Era of One-Party Dominance

What It Means: Congress ruled for years.

  • Why Congress Won: Freedom struggle, leaders like Nehru.
  • Decline: Corruption, party splits.
  • Why Important: Why Congress was strong.

3. Politics of Planned Development

What It Was: Economic growth plans.

  • Five-Year Plans: Government projects (e.g., dams).
  • Green Revolution: More food but favored rich areas.
  • Why Important: Green Revolution effects.

4. India’s External Relations

What It Covers: India’s global ties.

  • Non-Alignment: No US or Soviet side.
  • Key Events: Panchsheel (China), Pakistan wars.
  • Why Important: Foreign policy questions.

5. Challenges to and Restoration of the Congress System

What Happened: Congress lost, then returned.

  • Decline: Opposition grew (Janata Party 1977).
  • Restoration: Back in 1980 with Indira Gandhi.
  • Why Important: Shows political shifts.

6. The Crisis of Democratic Order

What It Was: Emergency (1975-77).

  • What Happened: Indira stopped rights, jailed foes.
  • Impact: Democracy fought back, got stronger.
  • Why Important: Emergency questions are common.

7. Rise of Popular Movements

What It Means: People fighting for rights.

  • Chipko Movement: Saved trees in Uttarakhand.
  • Narmada Bachao Andolan: Anti-dam in Gujarat.
  • Why Important: Short answer topics.

8. Regional Aspirations

What It Covers: State issues.

  • Punjab: Sikh demands, violence.
  • Kashmir: Status conflicts.
  • Northeast: Independence demands.
  • Why Important: Map or long questions.

9. Recent Developments in Indian Politics

What It’s About: Modern politics.

  • Coalition Politics: Parties team up (NDA, UPA).
  • Issues: Caste, religion, new parties (AAP).
  • Why Important: Links to today.

Sample Questions and Answers

Multiple-Choice Questions (MCQs)

1. Which country is not a permanent member of the UN Security Council?

  • A) China B) France C) Germany D) Russia
  • Answer: C) Germany
  • Explanation: Permanent members are US, UK, Russia, China, France.

2. What was the goal of the Non-Aligned Movement?

  • A) Support US B) Stay neutral C) Join Soviet Union D) Fight wars
  • Answer: B) Stay neutral
  • Explanation: India didn’t pick a side in the Cold War.

Short Answer Questions (2-4 Marks)

1. What is hegemony? Give an example.

Answer: Hegemony is when one country controls others due to its strength. Example: US influences the world with wars and trade rules.

2. What was the Green Revolution’s impact on India?

Answer: It increased food production with new seeds, but helped rich farmers more and hurt the environment.

Long Answer Questions (6 Marks)

1. Explain the causes and effects of the Cold War.

Answer: Causes: US and Soviet Union had opposite ideas (capitalism vs. communism) and wanted power. They didn’t trust each other, built weapons. Effects: World split into two groups, fought via other countries (e.g., Vietnam), ended in 1991 with US on top.

2. What happened during the Emergency in India, and how did it affect democracy?

Answer: In 1975, Indira Gandhi declared Emergency, stopped rights, jailed opponents. It hurt democracy, but after 1977, people voted her out, making democracy stronger.

Map-Based Question

Locate the state where the Chipko Movement started.

Answer: Uttarakhand

Explanation: Started in the 1970s to save trees.
